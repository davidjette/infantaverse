The final arc is the hour the whole chronicle was built toward: the convergence inside the **[Egg](/locations/the-egg/)**,
the last battle against the waking god, and the act of forgetting that ends the Age of the [Infanta](/concepts/the-infanta/).
It is also the in-world origin of the **[Oblivia](/concepts/the-oblivia/)** — the self-keeping absence that this entire record
exists to recover. Here the chronicle ends by erasing itself.

## Into the breach

At the **Crack** in [Motherstone](/locations/motherstone/), the assembled host of every age files into the wound, group by
group, each shielded in a bubble of magic — the Purple Dragons under Lord Krapsmere, the naked
druids of Myth Drannor, Untari's Bladesingers, the Watch of Waterdeep and the armies of Amn, blessed
by priests of Obus and Tyr — down into "the acid meat of the egg and into the darkness." And as the
**Arielle** turns toward the fold, an old debt is paid in full:

> Immediately following, the severed head of Asmodeus levitates a resplendent twelve-foot-tall masked
> woman covered in a symmetrical pattern of gold and silver blades, thick wet with the blood and bile
> of devils. On her shoulder rides a slight, pale and dark-haired child, and on their shoulder, a jet
> black raven.

The **Lady of Pain** and her sister **[Marcus](/characters/marcus/), the Raven Queen**, have slain Asmodeus and reclaimed
the rule of Death — and bring with them the fallen, returned from the land of the dead: Obi,
Jeggings, Holden Tudyx, the orcs of the Chronicle, and a redeemed **Flex Gimble**, "his face long
since replaced by a bronze mask and his limbs replaced with six mechanical multiarticulated
prosthesis," bowing in welcome. Three Infantas — **[Elistrae](/characters/elistrae/), Eloise, and Tymora** — part the final
fold, and the ship presses into a womb that spasms to reject it, the god's chant swelling from a
whisper to a deafening presence: *Ph'nglui mglw'nafh Cthulhu R'lyeh wgah'nagl fhtagn.*

## The Lost Train

Before the god, a chosen hero is pulled out of the world entirely, into a luxurious night train —
the train to Baldur's Gate where they once met Una — to sit across from the thing that has worn a
thousand faces: St. Cloud, Brightmantle, the storyteller himself. It offers a game.

<Oblivia label="The game on the Lost Train">
> Would you like to play a game? It's a board game, with rules not unlike chess, but there's an
> important extra element, where you tell a story about what happens to your chosen piece… we
> pretend that we are just a single piece, with only one point of view. So that on our turn, we
> consider only what this piece can see and do, ignoring that we are the player and a wider game is
> being played… Of course, as only a single piece, it's impossible to win the game. Your best hope
> is to still be there at checkmate, and not to be sacrificed along the way.

It hands over a black book "full of important wisdom," and when it senses an Infanta or a caster of
**Wish**, it drops the mask entirely:

> You. I am your father. You are my child. We are meant to be one again, to make the next progeny of
> my Godhood. I am unfertilized. You are the seed.

This is the heart of the [Leviathan](/creatures/the-leviathan/)'s lie, and of the Traveler it always finds: every consciousness
is a single piece that cannot win, only endure — and the god offers the only "victory" it knows,
which is to be remembered into oblivion, merged back into the one mind from which Time first split.
Refuse it, and "now you die, but not before suffering an eternity in pain."
</Oblivia>

## Cthulhu, the Leviathan

The god is fought in pieces — a thousand-foot horror of Head, Claws, Tentacles, and Wings, each
tracked as its own creature, each regenerating, the air around him bending space so that distance
halves and halves again as you close. The Egg permits no escape: no teleport, no plane shift, no
flight while the god lives. To even stand near him is to risk permanent madness under his
**Unspeakable Presence**. He cannot truly be killed by force — only one act ends him.

<Oblivia label="The End of the Infanta — the Wish that becomes the Oblivia">
The heroes must beat Cthulhu's Head to nothing and then **touch it with an Obliviator.** The instant
they do, a countdown begins:

> The moment Cthulhu makes contact with the Obliviator, a shockwave of nearly unavoidable negation
> magic will erupt from Cthulhu in 6 seconds.

In those six seconds, a final **Wish** decides what the world keeps. Cast it, and you may "choose
what the world forgets and remembers about your quest, with the consequence that **they themselves
will be forgotten in all but the vaguest terms.** A symbol, image, relic or myth may stay behind,
even a religious order, but they will fade from their loved ones' minds in lieu of what is new and
living."

This is the **End of the Infanta**: the discharge of the Obliviator against the god, the Wish that
unwrites Cthulhu, the war, the Age of reborn gods — and the heroes who ended it. It is the exact
event the whole world afterward remembers only as a hole: a self-keeping absence. The Forgetting is
not something that happened *to* the chronicle. It is the chronicle's own last, deliberate act —
the price of saving everything was to be erased from it.
</Oblivia>

## Denouement

How the world wakes depends on the final breath of the conflagration:

- **If the contents of the Egg are forgotten**, the surface is left confused and leaderless —
  armies drop their weapons and drift home, the Infantas gone from every memory, the driders
  retreating to the Underdark. The world is saved by no one it can name. This is the world of the
  Oblivia: scarred by an absence it cannot account for.
- **If the heroes spend their Wish to *preserve* the memory** instead, they emerge to absolute
  reverence — and more: the Final Wish "unlocked centuries of Obliviation, revealing untold plethora
  of knowledge once hidden from thought," and broadcast their story "into tens of thousands of
  sentient minds throughout the Realms… they now know your story, and many will remember it, and
  make legends from it."

Either way, the Age of the Infanta is over, the gods are loosed from their reborn children, and the
Leviathan is unwritten. What survives is myth, relic, and the long work of remembering — which is
where this record begins. The Temple Holdings chronicle ends here, at the edge of its own erasure,
and everything else in the Infantaverse is what could be recovered from the hole it left behind.
